Setbacks and Resistance

[Sen. Everett Dirksen, 1965 April 14]

Resistance Within the Senate

To convince liberal Republicans to oppose the Fair Housing Act, Senator Everett Dirksen of Illinois proposed a compromise (that was later implemented) that would reduce the bill’s coverage to 80% of the nation’s housing, eliminate the HUD’s authority to enforce it, and lower penalties for violations.

Southern senators, who held key positions in Congress, repeatedly thwarted advancements of civil rights. Senator Sam Ervin of North Carolina proposed an amendment taking out reference to “race, color, religion, or national origin.” He and other Southerners wanted to rid the bill of any civil rights connotation, leaving it vague and ineffective.

Real Estate Boards

The National Association of Real Estate Brokers’ 1924 code of ethics stated that “a Realtor should never be instrumental in introducing into a neighborhood… members of any race or nationality… whose presence will clearly be detrimental to property values in that neighborhood.” The referral to “race or nationality” was not removed until 1950. In 1927, the Chicago Real Estate Board developed a restrictive covenant, a private contract between property owners in an area who agreed to not sell homes to non-Caucasians. This served as a model for neighborhoods and real estate boards across the nation. The widely-used underwriting manual of the Federal Housing Administration explained that “If a neighborhood is to retain stability, it is necessary that properties shall continue to be occupied by the same social and racial classes.”
