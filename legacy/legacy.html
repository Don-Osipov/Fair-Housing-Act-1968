Legacy

[The family of J.D. Shelley pictured at their home, 4600 Labadie Ave. From left, Mrs. Minerva Coleman, a daughter, holding her son Jesse Jr.; Chatlee, Mrs. Shelley, Mr. Shelley, Leatha and J.D. Jr. - 1948]

Since the Fair Housing Act has been passed, it has prevented many cases of housing discrimination. Over half a million discrimination complaints have been made since 1996, resulting in almost $1 billion in compensation to victims. Since 1991, over 70,000 units of housing have been made accessible to people of color and disability.

[Residential segregation leads to school segregation through school zoning, Tomas E. Monarrez, 2017]

To this day, the biggest problem of the Fair Housing Act is the government’s inability to enforce it. The Dirksen compromise made during the drafting of the bill helped it pass, but greatly reduced its effectiveness by saying that although the HUD could enforce rules against discrimination, it could only do so through “conference, conciliation, and persuasion.” These changes made it difficult for the ideals of the Fair Housing Act to be put into practice. Explicit racial exclusion is illegal, but economic exclusionary zoning laws that disproportionately impact people of color still exist in many major American cities. As of 2019, black home ownership rates are at only 42% compared to the 73% of whites.

[Low-cost African American housing that maintained racial segregation (Savannah, GA)]

Although the Fair Housing Act provided new housing opportunities for African Americans, many government-subsidized houses created or expanded existing racially segregated neighborhoods. This practice is still common, resulting in many blacks not having the same opportunities as their white peers.
