Allies of the Civil Rights Act of 1968

[Martin Luther King Jr. during the Chicago Freedom March, 1966]

Martin Luther King Jr.

King was a major civil rights leader. His actions directly led to the forming of the nation’s first fair housing organization, the Leadership Council for Metropolitan Open Communities, which worked to credit African American communities and shed light on the discrimination of real estate companies.

His revolutionary visions of equality remain with us and inspire reform to this day.

[President Lyndon Johnson and civil rights leaders in the White House office, Jan. 18, 1964.]

Lyndon B. Johnson

President Lyndon B. Johnson brought into his presidency a vision of an equal country, which he tried to create using his "Great Society" legislation. He empowered action initiatives to combat poverty and placed black leaders in charge.

Johnson’s commitment to ending discrimination produced many civil rights bills and eventually the Fair Housing Act, signed April 11, 1968.

“Fair housing for all—all human beings who live in this country—is now a part of the American way of life.”
- Lyndon B. Johnson

[Walter Mondale, shortly after he became vice president, 1977]
[Senator Edward Brooke, 6 Jan. 1967]

Senators Edward Brooke and Walter Mondale

Senators Mondale and Brooke were co-authors of Fair Housing Act of 1968 and key players in its success.

Edward Brooke was a leading advocate against housing discrimination, repeatedly proposing stronger provisions to the Fair Housing Act. In 1969 the Brooke Amendment was passed, limiting the rent expenditure of tenants in federally assisted housing program to 25% of their income. During Nixon’s presidency, he opposed administration attempts to weaken the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission.

"We can pray today that the death of the nonviolent leader will not bring violence to life. In the days ahead, we must act to fulfill King's dream. . . . It is up to Congress today to lend powerful support to the argument of black moderates by immediately passing the 1968 civil rights bill, and by moving quickly to provide employment and housing opportunities for all blacks and whites."
- Walter Mondale

[Children Representing the NAACP during the Jim Crow Era, 1918]

The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People

During the civil rights era, NAACP prioritized legislative and judicial solutions over King’s direct action. However, they provided legal aid and posted bail for members of protest groups. Their advocacy helped pass the 1964 Civil Rights Act, and continues to remain critical to the African American community.
